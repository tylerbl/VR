Immersive Technologies — Looking Forward in the Digital Scholarship Commons

Tyler Andrew Blackman

“For me, VR’s greatest value is as a palate cleanser. Everyone becomes used to the most basic experiences of life and our world, and we take them for granted. Once your nervous system adapts to a virtual world, however, and then you come back, you have a chance to experience being born again in microcosm. The most ordinary surface, cheap wood or plain dirt, is bejeweled in infinite detail for a short while. (Jaron Lanier in Dawn of the New Everything, 2017)

“Might there be possibilities within immersive media for creating shared experiences that imagine pathways towards a preferred future?” (Julia Scott-Stevenson in Virtual Futures: A Manifesto for Immersive Experiences, 2019)

 

Jaron Lanier is credited with coining the term Virtual Reality (VR) in the 1980’s. Now many years later Lanier writes that VR’s true revolutionary power is in what happens after you take the headset off — how the experience of VR changes how you see the world and what you might do in the world.

 

Julia Scott Stevenson’s vision for immersive media takes up a similar philosophy by practically asking how immersive experiences can be shared, imaginative, and work towards a more desirable future.

Key Question: How can immersive experiences change how we come to know and act in the world for the better?

 

This question should be the core exploration of the Digital Scholarship Commons (DSC) in advancing VR and other immersive technologies as tools for pedagogy and research. The following document serves as an early form of visioning — it is not meant to be prescriptive but explorative. It consists of key questions, goals, and resources surrounding immersive media (primarily involving current tools the DSC has at its disposal).

 

Immersive Media: Inventory and Tool Library

The DSC’s immersive media tool library is growing. The following is the core list of tools available:

·      Oculus Rift VR Headset (VR Room)

·      Oculus Go Standalone VR Headset

·      4x Google Daydream VR Headsets

·      3x Google Cardboard VR Headsets

·      Insta360 One X Camera (360-degree photography/videography)

·      Sennheiser AMBEO (3D spatial audio capture)

 

Each of these tools has their strengths and weaknesses. As of right now the Oculus Rift is the most institutionalized having established procedures for use, safety guidelines, setup instructions, current and past projects, partnerships with faculty, etc. These tools are also integrative. For example, the Insta360 One X camera and Sennheiser AMBEO are useful for creating VR video content.

Making VR Approachable

 

VR is still daunting to newcomers.

Often students are hesitant to try and use VR, especially for the first time. This can be related to fears of getting motion sick, doing something embarrassing, of not looking competent with the technology in front of their peers, or being generally concerned about how VR might affect them during and after use.

 

VR’s first impression is important for it to be successfully used as an educational or research tool.

 

Key Question: How Can We Make VR More Approachable?

 

1.    What is VR? Despite the growth of VR many people have never tried it or never seen it in use. The DSC should prioritize a digitally accessible introduction to VR. This document should be written in lay language, debunk misconceptions about VR, acknowledge and respect possible user concerns, and provide additional information when necessary.

 

2.    Why VR? The DSC currently does not have a strong explanation for why university community members would want to use VR. The DSC should prioritize a statement, preferably on the DSC VR webpage, as to why VR or other immersive technologies might be of interest. When possible, this should provide examples of collaborations or projects (ongoing or completed).

 

3.    Don’t Just Do, Do Together: Is it more comforting to have someone joining with you in VR? How can the individual experience of VR be brought to a shared experience? There are “multiplayer” and “shared” experiences available when using multiple devices such as the Oculus Go, Google DayDream, or Google Cardboard. Sometimes these scenarios involve one person in VR and another using a mobile device in combination (e.g. Covert). Others involve VR itself as a shared space for social interaction (e.g. Alt Space VR). Should the DSC explore social VR more in-depth? What is its creative, pedagogical, or research potential?

 

4.    Reviewing Ethical Guidelines: UVic has at least two VR rooms on campus (DSC and BEC). The technology is rapidly changing and presents new ethical questions and concerns. For example, do VR headsets collect user data and in what ways? Also, if VR is truly more engaging than other kinds of media then how should content or projects be vetted as acceptable and consistent with the DSC’s values?

 

5.    Markers of Difference: VR and immersive technologies remain both promising and limiting because of real challenges felt through the intersections of ability, gender, race, and sexuality. For example, some research suggests women experience motion sickness more often than men. What is the DSC’s role in decolonizing technologies and opening up the digital as an accessible category? This is a practical question! For example, should the DSC webpage have a territorial acknowledgement? Should the DSC have a statement on accessibility and inclusivity through technologies and what the space represents as a practiced “commons”?

Resources

As VR and other immersive media continue to grow so does the number of enthusiast and creative content available online. The following are some notable examples for those who want to dig deeper. They are in no particular order.

Voices of VR Podcast: In-depth interviews with VR experts from many backgrounds.

Virtual Reality Toronto: A Canadian specific industry VR conference.

The Public VR Lab: A group dedicated to civic media and inclusivity in VR.

Report on Hate in Social VR: Report outlining concerns on hate and discrimination in social VR.

Disability and VR: A primer article on VR for individuals with disabilities.

Aboriginal Technologies in Cyberspace: Aboriginally determined research-creation network. VR artwork and games have been an output of this group in the past.

7 Things to Know About VR: An example resource for introducing VR in the library.

VR in Libraries: Primer article on VR in libraries.

A Vision for Women in VR: A document for addressing gendered imbalances in the immersive creative industries.

A Manifesto for Immersive Experiences: A clear vision on what to prioritize in immersive media.

Review of the Insta 360 One X: A great example of the potential for 360 video.

VR Views: embedding 360 photos or video into websites.

Imbedding 360-degree content on WordPress: Wordpress allows for 360-degree content to be imbedded on their webpages fairly easily.

Learning with 360-degree video: Good blog on the value of 360-degree video with tips.

Notes on 360 Journalism: More tips on 360-degree video.

Beggingers Guide to Spatial Audio: Tips on creating spatial audio.
